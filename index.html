1. The Old Battlefield: E-commerce Models

To understand why ONDC was created, we first need to understand the two dominant models of e-commerce and how they shaped the market in India. The government's regulations aimed to create fairness, but powerful platforms found ways to maintain their edge, creating the problems ONDC now seeks to solve.

Inventory Model

The platform owns the products, warehouses them, and sells them directly to you. Think of it as a single, giant online store.

Control: Full control over pricing, inventory, and customer experience.

Responsibility: Manages the entire supply chain from procurement to delivery.

Regulation: India banned this model for foreign e-commerce giants like Amazon and Flipkart to protect local retailers.

Marketplace Model

The platform is a digital mall, connecting many independent sellers with customers. It doesn't own the goods.

Control: Indirect influence via platform policies, algorithms, and seller services.

Responsibility: Facilitates the transaction, but sellers handle their own inventory and fulfillment.

The Loophole: While allowed, dominant platforms were accused of favoring specific large sellers, effectively creating pseudo-inventory models and influencing prices, thus circumventing the spirit of the law.

2. The Unfair Fight: Monopoly & Local Sellers

The failure of early regulations to create a truly level playing field led to significant problems. Small, local sellers found it nearly impossible to compete, while a few dominant platforms gained immense power to shape the market, often to the detriment of both small businesses and consumer choice.

Invisible Sellers

Local sellers get lost in a sea of listings, unable to afford the marketing or algorithmic boosts needed for visibility.

Price Wars & Predatory Pricing

Large platforms can sustain deep discounts and losses to eliminate smaller competitors, eventually leading to a monopoly where they can set any price.

Logistical Nightmares

High costs for warehousing and delivery, especially outside major cities, make it hard for small sellers to compete on speed and price.

Market Power Imbalance

  • Dominant Platforms: Control ~90% of market visibility and customer reach
  • Local Sellers: Limited to ~25% effective market access despite having quality products
  • Algorithm Bias: Search results favor platforms' preferred sellers
  • Commission Burden: High fees (15-30%) eat into small seller profits
  • Data Monopoly: Customer data locked within platform ecosystems

3. A New Alliance: The ONDC Solution & Architecture

In response to these challenges, the Indian government launched ONDC. It's not another app or platform, but a revolutionary open network—a set of rules and tools that allows any buyer and seller application to talk to each other. The goal is to break down the walled gardens of dominant platforms and democratize digital commerce.

ONDC Key Advantages

  • Lower Commissions: Reduced fees for sellers (35% impact)
  • Wider Market Reach: Access to entire network ecosystem (30% impact)
  • Better Prices: Increased competition benefits consumers (20% impact)
  • Hyperlocal Delivery: Faster, cheaper local services (15% impact)
  • Data Democracy: Sellers own their customer relationships
  • Innovation Freedom: Open standards encourage new solutions

ONDC Charges & Costs

ONDC Charges: The ONDC network itself is a non-profit initiative by the government and initially operated on a zero-commission model. However, it **plans to introduce a nominal fee of Rs 1.5 per transaction for orders above Rs 250**.

Participant Fees: Individual network participants (Buyer Apps, Seller Apps, Logistics Providers, etc.) may charge their own fees or commissions for the services they provide. These are determined by the respective apps and not by ONDC.

Infrastructure Costs: ONDC's infrastructure costs are relatively low due to its open-source, decentralized nature. It relies on existing digital public infrastructure (like UPI for payments) and a federated model where participants bear the cost of their own applications and services, rather than a single entity building and maintaining a monolithic platform.

Understanding ONDC's Technical Foundation

🛠️ What ONDC Actually Is
  • Not a single app or platform, but a set of open protocols
  • Built on the Beckn Protocol - think of it as the "language" that allows different e-commerce apps to talk to each other
  • A network of networks that connects existing and new apps
  • Government-backed digital public infrastructure (like UPI for payments)
💻 How Developers Can Join
  • Beckn Protocol Server: Ready-to-use software that helps apps connect to ONDC
  • White-Label Solutions: Pre-built app templates for buyers, sellers, and logistics
  • Open API Specifications: Detailed guides on developer portals and Swagger Hub
  • Cloud Infrastructure: Uses Google Cloud services for reliability and scale
🚀 Real-World Impact

Imagine WhatsApp, Telegram, and Signal users being able to message each other seamlessly. That's what ONDC does for e-commerce - it breaks down the walls between different shopping apps, allowing customers on Paytm to buy from sellers using completely different platforms like Mystore or GoFrugal.

For Developers: Build once, reach the entire network
For Businesses: Access customers from any buyer app
For Consumers: More choice, better prices, local options

4. ONDC Progress & Challenges

While ONDC's vision is powerful, the journey is filled with significant hurdles. Success is not guaranteed and depends on overcoming technical complexities, building user trust, and navigating resistance from the very giants it aims to challenge. The data reveals a cautious but growing ecosystem.

ONDC Transaction Growth & Key Metrics

Since its launch, ONDC has seen steady growth in transactions and participant engagement, indicating growing momentum in its mission to democratize digital commerce.

📈 Transaction Growth Journey

  • March 2023 (Launch): Started with pilot transactions in select cities
  • September 2023: Expanded to multiple categories and cities
  • March 2024: Reached significant milestone with sustained growth
  • September 2024: Crossed 10 Crore cumulative transactions
  • March 2025: Achieved over 20.4 Crore total transactions

Growth Rate: From pilot to 20+ Crore transactions in just 2 years shows promising adoption trajectory.

👥 User Experience Challenges

  • 54% find it cumbersome: Navigation and discovery issues
  • 46% are satisfied: Appreciate the concept and benefits
  • Key Issues: Complex onboarding, app fragmentation
  • Improvement Areas: Unified search, simpler interfaces

Early adopter feedback from May 2025 surveys shows room for UX improvements.

🔐 Adoption & Trust Barriers

  • 35% cite poor service: Inconsistent quality across network
  • 30% face tech hurdles: Integration and reliability issues
  • Trust Building: Slow process without major brand backing
  • Quality Control: Ensuring standards across diverse participants

Growth is steady but adoption acceleration requires addressing these concerns.

🏭 Industry Resistance

  • 26% expect active resistance: From established platforms
  • 34% see business model disruption: Threatens existing monopolies
  • Competitive Response: Platforms may create their own networks
  • Regulatory Support: Government backing crucial for success

Industry expects significant pushback as ONDC gains momentum and threatens established players.
